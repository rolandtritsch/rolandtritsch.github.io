The pillars of a sustainable energy future - A fireside chat

2023-03-12 Management Personal Software-Craftmanship Climate Environment Innovation Politics Thoughts ChatGPT

This blog post is interesting in more than one way, because …

  • I am going to discuss a topic that is deer to my heart: What do we need to do to get to a better place with respect to a sustainable energy future?
  • And I am going to use a new approach: Where I am first going to articulate my thoughts and then I am going to complement it with a fireside chat that I had with chatGPT about the topics at hand.

Here we go …

Thoughts

These days we talk about sustainablilty and renewable energy. A lot. And rightfully so. We need to solve this problem. We must.

But a lot of the discussions are just focused on creating the energy (using solar-panels and wind-farms).

I think that solving the problem to have enough energy at any given point in time during the day (e.g. when the sun is not shinning and when the wind is now blowing (at least not where the energy is needed/consumed)) whereever it is needed, whenever it is needed, needs three pillars:

  • Create/Produce the energy
  • Transport/Distribute the energy
  • Store/Buffer the energy

I do not want to call the produce part a solved problem, but we know a lot about it and the research/progress to make it even better looks good.

The other two a less at the forefront of our thinking, but you just have to look at a picture of the hours of sunshine in Europe to see/understand that energy will not get produced equally. Some areas will have more than others and at times so much that it could/should be shipped some place else.

Here is the problem: Our current grids are not up to it!

With our current grid infrastructure transporting/distributing energy over long distances and country boundaries is not effective/efficient. The losses are too high.

But there is hope and there are big dreams.

A couple of years ago Desertec tried to build a large solar power plant in the Sahara and ship the electricity up north through the Mediterian Sea with/on high-voltage direct-current (HVDC) lines. For now the project is struggling to continue to get/find funding and to overcome political back-pressure, but efforts like this spark the right kind of questions and will power/push the right kind of reearch: How can we transport/distribute electricity over long distances?

By now the two most promising areas of research and experimentation are large high-voltage direct-current (HVDC) grids and (just hot of the press) super-grids that use super-conducting cables.

Based on these technologies we could ship solar electricity north during day and wind electricity south during the night.

The last pillar is a weird one. Mainly because I am a software-engineer.

I have build large-scale, high-throughput, distributed, event-based SaaS-backends all my life. And we all know that to make a system like this work you have to decouple the producer and the consumer of the events by means of an event-bus. To ensure that the overall system is available and reliable and resilient it needs to be asynchrounus. A glitch in one part of the system needs to be/get dampended by the system, not amplified.

You need buffers and queues.

Right now our grids do not have any (or enough) buffers and queues. More or less electricity needs to get produced the second it gets consumed.

One of the most popular attempts to fix this is pumped hydroelectric energy storage (PHES), where water is pumped up into a reservoir while you have to much power and then use the water to drive turbines when you need it. But that approach has a couple of problems that come with it: Low effiency, water evaporating (in countries with high temperatures; you can try to mitigate this somehow/a little bit by covering up the water) and the buffer being in the middle of the grid and not at the edge of the grid.

Meet Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G)! It is a new standard that allows electric cars to get charged at home with a home charger, but also power the home (or by extension the grid). Just imagine ten million electric cars plugged in at any given point in time and you specify that by 09:00 in the morning you want 50% charge, but that until then the battery can be used to power the house (the average daily power consumption of an Irish household is ~15 kW/h; the battery of the new Volvo EX90 is ~100 kW/h; means with 50% of the battery capacity you can run the house for 3-4 days) or buffer the grid.

That’s a pretty big battery/buffer.

But it gets even better. So lets say that the solution is to build a next generation super-grid that can transport large amounts of energy with little loss and can tolerate and decouple the fluctuations between the producers and consumers of sustainable energy by means of (levels and layers of) buffer-batteries.

That means you need a lot of batteries (way beyond of what I described above). And big ones. And affortable ones.

And we all know that Li-Ion batteries have (at least) one disadvantage: You need rare earth metals to build them!

So what we need to solve this problem are batteries that have a lot of capacity, are reasonably efficient, not too expensive to build and do not need rare earth metals.

Two more things to consider: They do not need to be light-weight and they do not need to be small (because they will be stationary (e.g. in the basement of your house or in a gym-sized building that powers/buffers a part of a city)).

Lukily right now lots of research goes into solving that problem. One interesting take on this are Organic Flow Batteries. They have a lot of the properties we are looking for to decouple the producers and consumers of energy (in space/by distance and time).

Flow batteries are a type of rechargeable battery that uses two tanks of liquid electrolyte to store energy. The electrolytes are pumped through a cell stack, where they undergo a redox reaction to produce electrical energy. Flow batteries are known for their ability to store large amounts of energy and for their long cycle life, making them well-suited for stationary energy storage applications such as power grid management, renewable energy storage, and backup power systems.

There are several different types of flow batteries, including:

Zinc-bromine flow batteries: Zinc-bromine flow batteries use a zinc-bromine electrolyte system. Zinc ions are dissolved in one electrolyte tank, while bromine is dissolved in the other. When the electrolytes are pumped through the cell stack, zinc ions react with bromine to produce electricity.

Iron-chromium flow batteries: Iron-chromium flow batteries use an iron-chromium electrolyte system. Iron ions are dissolved in one electrolyte tank, while chromium ions are dissolved in the other. When the electrolytes are pumped through the cell stack, the two ions react to produce electricity.

Organic flow batteries: Organic flow batteries use organic molecules as the electrolyte. These batteries can be made from renewable materials and are relatively environmentally friendly.

Hybrid flow batteries: Hybrid flow batteries combine different types of electrolytes to achieve better performance and cost-effectiveness.

Each type of flow battery has its own unique characteristics, advantages, and limitations. The choice of flow battery depends on the specific application and requirements, such as cost, efficiency, energy density, and safety.

What are the alternatives to HVDC?

The main alternative to high-voltage direct current (HVDC) transmission is the use of traditional alternating current (AC) transmission lines. AC transmission lines have been the standard method for transmitting electricity over long distances for many years, and are still widely used today. The advantages of AC transmission include:

Cost: AC transmission lines are generally less expensive to build than HVDC transmission lines.

Compatibility: AC transmission lines are compatible with most existing electrical infrastructure, including transformers and switchgear.

Simplicity: AC transmission lines are simpler to operate and maintain than HVDC transmission lines.

However, AC transmission lines have some limitations when it comes to transmitting electricity over long distances, particularly when transmitting electricity between different regions or countries. Some of the main limitations of AC transmission include:

Electrical losses: AC transmission lines experience higher electrical losses than HVDC transmission lines when transmitting electricity over long distances. This can result in lower efficiency and higher costs.

Grid stability: AC transmission lines can sometimes lead to instability in the electrical grid, particularly when transmitting large amounts of electricity over long distances. This can result in power outages and other disruptions.

Limited capacity: AC transmission lines have limited capacity to transmit large amounts of electricity over long distances. This can limit the amount of renewable energy that can be transmitted from remote locations to areas of high demand.

Overall, while AC transmission lines remain a viable option for transmitting electricity over shorter distances, HVDC transmission lines are generally considered to be a more efficient and reliable option for transmitting electricity over long distances.
